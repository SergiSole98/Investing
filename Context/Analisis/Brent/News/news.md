## News: Brent Crude Oil
Scope: impact-driven items (operational, regulatory, strategic, commodity/macro), recognized sources only, horizon 4h–7d (project); sources within last 7 days

- [2026-03-28] Iran war-hit oil prices will soon rise if Hormuz stays shut — Source: CNBC
  Brent crude settled at $112.57/bbl on March 27 (+4.22%), its highest close since mid-2022, as the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively shut to almost all non-Iranian-linked vessels. Since the conflict began on Feb. 28, only 21 tankers have transited the route versus 100+ daily prior; approximately 22 vessels remain anchored awaiting clearance. Analysts warn prices will climb further unless a diplomatic resolution emerges before Trump's April 6 energy-attack pause expires, with supply losses from the Hormuz blockade now dwarfing any previous disruption in history. The Dubai physical price is up 76% to ~$126/bbl since Feb. 27, confirming extreme spot market tightness.

- [2026-03-27] Oil prices close at highest level since 2022 as Iran negotiations fail to ease supply fears — Source: CNBC
  WTI rose 5.46% to $99.64/bbl and Brent gained 4.22% to $112.57/bbl on March 27, as markets concluded that Iran's rejection of direct talks and continuation of the Hormuz blockade outweighed ceasefire optimism. Iranian state media confirmed Tehran would reject a U.S. ceasefire offer and had laid out its own conditions for ending the conflict. Iraq's production remains collapsed at ~1.2 mb/d versus 4.3 mb/d pre-war, Kuwait refineries sustained drone damage, and Saudi Arabia shut Ras Tanura precautionarily, sustaining the structural supply vacuum.

- [2026-03-26] Trump extends pause on striking Iranian energy plants to April 6 at 20:00 ET — Source: Bloomberg / CNBC
  President Trump extended the deadline for U.S. strikes on Iranian power grid and energy infrastructure to Sunday April 6, 2026 at 20:00 ET — the second extension since the initial 48-hour ultimatum issued on March 22. Trump cited Iran allowing 10 oil ships through the Strait as a "present." The April 6 deadline is the single most important binary catalyst for Brent within the 4h–7d window: failure triggers direct strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure and likely pushes Brent above $120; a deal would compress the estimated $14–18/bbl risk premium.

- [2026-03-26] Israeli military kills IRGC Navy Commander Tangsiri in airstrike — Source: CNBC
  Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz confirmed that IRGC Navy Commander Alireza Tangsiri — the officer directly responsible for enforcing the Strait of Hormuz closure — was killed in an airstrike on March 26. His elimination could disrupt IRGC Navy command structure around the blockade operation but may equally trigger retaliatory escalation, adding an asymmetric near-term volatility premium to Brent. The loss of the operational commander of the Hormuz blockade is a material geopolitical event without direct precedent in the current conflict.

- [2026-03-26] Iran rejects direct U.S. talks; announces selective Hormuz transit for five nations — Source: CNBC / Al Jazeera
  Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi stated that exchanges through mediators "do not constitute negotiations with the U.S.," directly contradicting Trump's claim that talks were proceeding. Iran concurrently announced a selective transit system allowing ships from China, Russia, India, Iraq, and Pakistan through the Strait under IRGC vetting. Brent dropped from $108 to $105.85 intraday before rebounding; the reversal of Chinese ship access reported March 27 (Chinese vessels turned back) shows the vetting system is inconsistent and cannot be relied upon to restore normal flows.

- [2026-03-25] U.S.-Iran diplomatic signals send oil on whipsaw; Brent swings $10/bbl in two sessions — Source: Bloomberg / CNBC
  Trump's March 25 claim of active negotiations sent Brent -2.2% to $102.22/bbl; the following session reversed almost entirely (+5.66% to $108.01) as Iran denied the characterization. Bloomberg reported the episode as confirmation that the primary short-term price driver is the diplomatic/military status of the Hormuz blockade, with any credible diplomatic signal triggering multi-percent intraday moves. The pattern establishes a repeating volatility regime that persists until the April 6 deadline or a tangible Hormuz reopening.

- [2026-03-25] Russian oil finds buyers after U.S. waiver; Iranian cargoes lag — Source: Bloomberg
  Following the U.S. 30-day waivers on stranded Russian and Iranian crude, Russian barrels (~124 million barrels across 30 locations globally as of March 12) have moved to Asian buyers, but Iranian cargoes face banking and insurance barriers limiting actual relief. The gap between Brent futures (+36% since Feb. 27 to $113/bbl) and Dubai physical (+76% to ~$126/bbl) signals that the waivers are only partially offsetting physical supply tightness; prompt physical scarcity remains the dominant market signal.

- [2026-03-25] EIA weekly report: U.S. crude inventories +6.926 mb; fifth consecutive build — Source: EIA
  The EIA report released March 25 (week ending March 20) showed U.S. crude inventories rose 6.926 million barrels to 456.2 million barrels, far exceeding consensus of +0.5 million barrels. Cushing hub stocks rose 3.421 million barrels, the largest weekly increase since January 2023; distillates also built +3.032 million barrels. Five consecutive large builds indicate demand destruction from elevated prices and alternative supply routes are feeding U.S. storage, providing a bearish offset to geopolitical premiums; the next EIA report (April 1) will be the first post-April 6 deadline data signal.

- [2026-03-23] Goldman Sachs raises 2026 Brent forecast to $85/bbl average; near-term target $110–$115/bbl — Source: Bloomberg / Benzinga
  Goldman Sachs raised its near-term Brent forecast on March 23 to an average of $110/bbl for March–April 2026 (up from $98), and revised the 2026 full-year average to $85/bbl (up from $77), describing the Hormuz disruption as the largest-ever supply shock for the global crude market. Persian Gulf exports via vessel count data had fallen to ~3% of normal Hormuz flow levels, dwarfing the 1973 OPEC embargo and 1990 Gulf War in immediate flow impact. A six-month disruption scenario points to $135/bbl in Goldman's analysis; Q4 2026 price is only projected to normalize toward $66/bbl if Hormuz flows fully resume.

- [2026-03-23] Trump postpones strikes on Iranian power plants; Brent drops ~11% intraday — Source: CNBC
  Trump announced on March 23 that planned U.S. strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure were postponed for five days, describing talks as "productive," sending Brent -11% from ~$112 to ~$99.94/bbl intraday. Iran denied any direct negotiations were taking place and launched counter-strikes on Gulf targets. The episode was the largest single-session Brent decline in the current conflict and illustrates the magnitude of the risk premium: a genuine ceasefire or Hormuz reopening could release $14–18/bbl of embedded war premium within hours.

- [2026-03-20] U.S. Treasury issues 30-day sanctions waiver for ~140 million barrels of stranded Iranian crude — Source: CNBC / S&P Global
  OFAC issued a 30-day general license authorizing the purchase of Iranian-origin crude on vessels at sea, valid through April 19, 2026. Treasury Secretary Bessent stated Iran could not access the revenue while Washington maintained maximum financial pressure. The April 19 expiry creates a forward binary: non-renewal would re-tighten physical supply (bullish); renewal would extend the bearish relief but set a precedent of normalizing Iranian oil sales while the conflict continues.

- [2026-03-20] Iraq declares force majeure; Kuwait and Saudi refineries struck — Source: Reuters / CNBC / Benzinga
  Iraq's oil ministry declared force majeure on all foreign-operated fields on March 20, citing inability to export through the Strait; production collapsed from ~4.3 mb/d to ~1.2 mb/d. Drone strikes hit Kuwait's Mina Al-Ahmadi and Mina Abdullah refineries (~730,000 bpd combined capacity), and Saudi Arabia shut Ras Tanura (~550,000 bpd) precautionarily; Brent closed at $112.19/bbl (+3.26%). The physical infrastructure damage layer compounds the Hormuz blockade and raises the prospect of outages that would persist even after the strait reopens.

- [2026-03-11] IEA coordinated release of 400 million barrels; U.S. SPR contributing 172 million barrels — Source: Bloomberg / CNBC
  The IEA's 32 member nations agreed to the largest coordinated strategic reserve release in history: 400 million barrels total, with the U.S. DOE contributing 172 million barrels from the SPR over ~120 days (~1.4 mb/d). Despite the announcement, Brent continued to rise as Iran's new supreme leader declared the Strait must remain closed, with markets pricing the release as insufficient to offset over 20 mb/d of normal Hormuz flows. The release offsets an estimated 15% of lost Hormuz flows, providing only a partial price ceiling.

- [2026-03-01] OPEC+ agrees to 206,000 b/d production increase for April 2026; next decision April 5 — Source: Bloomberg / CNBC
  OPEC+ eight core producers confirmed a 206,000 b/d output increase for April, resuming the previously paused voluntary-cut unwind, with the next formal review meeting scheduled for April 5. The increase was chosen from a range of 137,000–548,000 b/d; Saudi Arabia and UAE hold the only meaningful spare capacity (~2 mb/d combined) but face Hormuz shipping constraints that limit actual physical delivery. Most of the April paper increase is not physically exportable under current Hormuz conditions, making the decision largely notional until the strait reopens.
