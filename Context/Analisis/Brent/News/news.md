## News: Brent Crude Oil
Scope: price-relevant items, horizon 4h–7d (project); sources within last 7 days

- [2026-03-16] Middle East oil benchmarks hit record highs as war cuts supply — Source: Reuters
  Dubai crude surged to $157.66/barrel on March 16, surpassing Brent's 2008 record of $147.50, as Middle Eastern crude exports fell to 11.665 million bpd from ~19 million bpd in February due to Iran's near-total blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. The IEA's 400 million barrel reserve release (authorized March 11) had not yet materially offset the disruption, with the Strait operating at roughly 5% of normal flows. The extreme tightness in Middle Eastern differentials signals a structural supply shortfall that keeps upward pressure on Brent in the near term.

- [2026-03-16] IEA chief says members could release more oil stocks "as and if needed" — Source: Reuters
  IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol confirmed that approximately 1.4 billion barrels remain in member reserves after the initial 400 million barrel collective release authorized March 11, and that a second tranche could be triggered. He described the supply situation as "very severe" and worse than the 1970s oil shocks. Any formal announcement of a second IEA release would be a significant bearish catalyst for Brent in the 4h–7d window.

- [2026-03-17] Gulf oil producers scramble to bypass Hormuz as Iran locks down the strait — Source: Reuters
  Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline (Petroline) reached a record 5.9 million bpd at the Yanbu terminal, approaching its 7 million bpd maximum capacity, while the UAE's Habshan-Fujairah pipeline (ADCOP) operated at its full 1.8 million bpd. However, the combined effective bypass capacity (≈4 million bpd, limited by port loading bottlenecks at Yanbu and Fujairah) covers only ~20–38% of the ~20 million bpd that normally transits Hormuz, leaving an unbridged gap of 10–16 million bpd. The persistent structural bypass limit means any rerouted volumes will be insufficient to fully offset the Hormuz closure, sustaining a supply-deficit premium in Brent pricing.

- [2026-03-18] Federal Reserve holds rates at 3.5–3.75%, projects only one cut in 2026 amid oil spike — Source: Reuters / NPR
  In an 11–1 vote on March 18, the FOMC left rates unchanged, revising its rate-cut path to a single cut for 2026 (from two-to-three previously) as the oil-driven inflation risk outweighed a weakening labor market (–92,000 jobs in February). Brent settled at $107.38/barrel the same day; market traders subsequently priced out Fed cuts entirely until 2027. A "higher for longer" Fed stance reduces demand-side support for oil over the medium term, though in the near term it may strengthen the USD, adding mild headwinds to Brent.

- [2026-03-19] EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report: commercial crude stocks rise 6.2 million barrels — Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA)
  For the week ending March 13, 2026, U.S. commercial crude oil inventories (ex-SPR) rose 6.2 million barrels to 449.3 million barrels, roughly 1% below the five-year seasonal average. The SPR stood at 415.4 million barrels; U.S. crude imports averaged 7.2 million bpd (+772,000 bpd vs. prior week). WTI was priced at $98.48/barrel on March 13. The above-expected stock build is a near-term bearish signal for Brent, partly reflecting the SPR releases beginning to flow into the market.

- [2026-03-20] Trump administration issues 30-day waiver lifting sanctions on ~140 million barrels of stranded Iranian crude — Source: CNN Politics / Fortune / OFAC
  The U.S. Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) issued a general license on March 20 allowing the purchase of approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude already loaded on vessels at sea, valid until April 19, 2026. The move—the third such adversary-oil sanction waiver in two weeks (after similar waivers on Russian oil)—was framed as emergency supply relief with Brent above $112/barrel and U.S. retail gasoline near $4/gallon. The injected volume (~140 million barrels over 30 days, ≈4.7 million bpd theoretical maximum) provides a material near-term bearish supply impulse if those cargoes reach buyers quickly.

- [2026-03-22] Trump issues 48-hour Hormuz ultimatum to Iran, threatening strikes on power plants — Source: CNBC / World Oil
  On March 22, President Trump gave Iran 48 hours to fully reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face U.S. airstrikes on Iranian power plants. The threat came amid continued near-total closure of the strait (≈5% of normal flows) and drove Brent to a weekly high of ~$112/barrel on March 20. The escalation rhetoric raises the tail risk of further supply infrastructure damage and sustained Hormuz closure, representing a near-term upside price catalyst if no diplomatic resolution materializes within the ultimatum window.

- [2026-03-23] Brent drops ~11% to $99.94 after Trump announces "productive conversations" with Iran and 5-day halt on strikes — Source: CNBC / Euronews / World Oil
  On March 23, Trump announced a temporary five-day halt on U.S. military strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure and described "very good and productive conversations" with Iran. Brent fell from $112 to $99.94/barrel (–≈11%) intraday, with WTI dropping to $88.13/barrel. Goldman Sachs had raised its near-term Brent forecast to $110 average for March–April. The ceasefire signal reduces the immediate Hormuz-closure risk premium, but the 5-day window is short and any breakdown in talks could rapidly reverse the move; market direction over the coming days hinges on whether negotiations advance or collapse.
