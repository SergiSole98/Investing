## Events: Brent Crude Oil
Scope: scheduled catalysts, horizon 0–7d; events with confirmed date 2026-03-26 to 2026-04-02

---

- [2026-03-26, ongoing] US–Iran Hormuz Strait Negotiation Window — Actor: White House / Trump Administration / Islamic Republic of Iran
  Trump's extended five-day pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure runs through approximately Saturday March 28 late evening ET. Iran continues to deny direct talks while communicating via Pakistani intermediaries. Consensus expects no formal agreement within the deadline. A confirmed ceasefire or Strait reopening framework would trigger a $5–15/bbl Brent selloff as the risk premium partially unwinds; a breakdown returning the US to active strikes on Iranian power plants would drive Brent sharply higher and add another layer to the existing ~$15–20/bbl geopolitical premium.

- [2026-03-26, 10:30 ET] EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report (week ending Mar 20) — Actor: US Energy Information Administration
  The EIA releases definitive US petroleum inventory data; the prior API print (Mar 25) showed a +2.3 mb crude build vs. a -1.3 mb consensus draw, following the prior EIA release (+6.2 mb). Consensus for this week's EIA report is now skewed toward another build given the large API surprise. A large confirmed crude build (bearish) would reinforce that demand destruction and IEA/sanctions waiver supply are bridging the physical deficit; an unexpected draw (bullish) would reaffirm the Hormuz disruption is still leaving global markets undersupplied and support higher Brent.

- [2026-03-27, 08:30 ET] PCE Price Index & Personal Income/Outlays (February 2026) — Actor: US Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA)
  February core PCE — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — with January printing +3.1% YoY (well above the 2.0% target). Consensus expects core PCE to remain elevated near 3.0–3.1% given energy price pass-through. A hotter-than-expected print reinforces higher-for-longer rates, strengthens the USD, and creates demand-side headwinds for Brent; a softer print would weaken the dollar and support risk appetite, amplifying Brent's upside within the supply-shock context.

- [2026-03-28, ~late evening ET] Trump–Iran Ultimatum Hard Deadline — Actor: White House / Trump Administration
  The five-day extension of Trump's Strait of Hormuz ultimatum expires. Trump has publicly committed to resuming threats of strikes on Iranian power plants if negotiations fail; Iran has not acknowledged direct talks. This is the most binary single event for Brent through April 2: a deal or partial reopening framework removes crisis premium and triggers a sharp selloff, while a breakdown and return to active military escalation would add a new supply-shock premium on top of the existing disruption.

- [2026-03-31, end of ICE session] ICE Brent Crude May 2026 Futures — Last Trading Day — Actor: ICE Futures Europe
  The May 2026 front-month ICE Brent contract expires; the roll into the June contract accelerates during the final week of March. In a backwardated market with elevated near-term premiums, position liquidation and roll-related flows can amplify intraday volatility on the last trading day. The prompt May–June spread will reprice in real time based on the outcome of the March 28 diplomatic deadline and any new supply developments; a failed deal would widen backwardation, while a ceasefire would compress it.

- [2026-04-01, 10:00 ET] ISM Manufacturing PMI (March 2026) — Actor: Institute for Supply Management
  The ISM Manufacturing PMI for March is released on the first business day of April. Prior reading was 49.8 (contraction, below the 50 threshold), against a consensus estimate of ~51.6–51.8. A further decline deeper into contraction would signal worsening industrial demand and weigh on crude oil consumption expectations, adding a demand-destruction overlay to the already elevated supply-shock environment; a recovery above 50 would partially offset demand-side bearish concerns.

- [2026-04-01, 10:30 ET] EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report (week ending Mar 27) — Actor: US Energy Information Administration
  The first EIA inventory data release post the March 28 diplomatic deadline. This report will be the first data point capturing whether the supply situation is improving or deteriorating after the ultimatum outcome. The market will watch for signs of a demand response or supply normalization. Continued large builds would cap Brent; a sharp draw would confirm persistent supply tightness despite IEA reserve releases and sanctions waivers.

- [2026-04-03, 08:30 ET] US Non-Farm Payrolls (March 2026) — Actor: US Bureau of Labor Statistics
  March NFP report; prior release (February) showed a net job loss of ~92,000, with Fed Chair Powell acknowledging job creation had "slowed to essentially zero." Consensus expects a modest rebound given seasonal adjustments, but the ongoing economic drag from elevated energy costs keeps downside risk elevated. A weak NFP print would amplify recession fears, weigh on demand outlook for crude, and pressure Brent; a strong beat would support risk assets and oil demand expectations.

- [2026-04-05] OPEC+ Eight-Country Ministerial Meeting — Actor: OPEC+ (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, Oman)
  The eight voluntary-cut OPEC+ countries meet to review market conditions, compliance, and the April 2026 production adjustment of +206,000 bpd (agreed March 1). Given the Hormuz crisis cutting effective Gulf production, the JMMC confirmed no plans to accelerate output increases before Q3 2026. The meeting could reverse or suspend the planned April output increase if markets remain disrupted; any decision to cut production further or delay the hike would be sharply bullish for Brent, while confirmation of the +206 kb/d increase as planned would provide mild near-term bearish pressure.

- [2026-04-07] EIA Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO) — April 2026 Release — Actor: US Energy Information Administration
  The EIA's monthly STEO includes updated price forecasts, inventory projections, and production outlooks. The March 2026 STEO incorporated the Hormuz disruption but the April edition will capture updated data from the crisis peak and any early resolution signals. A downward revision to global supply forecasts or an upward revision to Brent price projections would validate current spot levels and support continued elevated pricing; an upward revision to non-OPEC supply or demand destruction estimates would be mildly bearish.
