## Interests Map: Brent Crude Oil (ICE Brent May 2026)
Scope: structural incentives of identified smart money actors

### OPEC+ Core Eight
Bias: bullish | Confidence: high
Reason: Member states require high oil prices to fund national budgets; Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven is ~$80/bbl and current disruption provides a windfall above that level. The April 206,000 bpd increase is calibrated not to crash the price—OPEC+ explicitly retained flexibility to reverse. Structural incentive is to maintain $85–$100 price floor, not to see prices fall below $80.
Near-term deadline: April 2026 production quota review; any emergency meeting would be called if Brent falls below $85 for multiple sessions.

### IEA Member Governments
Bias: bearish | Confidence: high
Reason: The IEA's mandate is energy security—suppressing extreme oil prices to protect importing-nation economies. The 400M barrel release is an explicit price-suppression action. IEA chief stated further releases are possible "if needed," indicating a structural commitment to preventing prices from exceeding a tolerable level (likely $110–$120 threshold for additional action).
Near-term deadline: 120-day release window underway; weekly drawdown data released monthly; next IEA Oil Market Report due early April 2026.

### Saudi Aramco
Bias: bullish | Confidence: high
Reason: As a producer, Aramco benefits from elevated prices; however, as a state actor, Saudi Arabia also bears geopolitical risk from prolonged conflict. Aramco is maximizing bypass pipeline revenues via the Red Sea route. The structural constraint (1+ mb/d irrecoverable below peak Hormuz capacity via bypass) supports sustained backwardation and front-month price elevation.
Near-term deadline: Petroline bypass operating near maximum rated capacity (~5 mb/d); no expansion catalyst within 7 days.

### Hedge Funds / Managed Money
Bias: neutral / conflicted | Confidence: medium
Reason: After building the most bullish oil position since 2020 in early March, managers have aggressively reduced longs for six consecutive weeks (351k → 153k lots), suggesting trailing stop-outs and profit-taking rather than a conviction short. Current posture is event-driven: long if Iran talks collapse, reducing further on de-escalation signals.
Near-term deadline: Trump's 5-day deadline expires approximately Friday, March 27–28, 2026—directly within the analysis window and the highest-impact event for this category.

### US DOE / Strategic Petroleum Reserve
Bias: bearish | Confidence: high
Reason: Actively selling physical crude into spot markets over ~120 days from March 11. Mandate is explicit price suppression; the US contributed 172M barrels to the IEA release. Every barrel released adds supply to the spot market and compresses the front-month premium.
Near-term deadline: Ongoing release; no discrete near-term trigger, but weekly SPR drawdown data published by EIA creates recurring modest bearish signal.

### Goldman Sachs / Morgan Stanley / JPMorgan (Swap Dealers)
Bias: neutral / bullish (skewed) | Confidence: medium
Reason: As swap dealers, they hold net long exposure through commodity index products (e.g., GSCI, BCOM rebalances). Their structural role is market-making, not directional—however, index roll activity in late March (end-of-month approaching) creates near-term mechanical selling in front-month and buying in second-month contracts, temporarily compressing spot spreads.
Near-term deadline: Month-end commodity index rebalancing period begins approximately March 25–31; Goldman Sachs Roll occurs late March into early April.

### Saudi Arabia Public Investment Fund (PIF)
Bias: bullish | Confidence: medium
Reason: PIF depends on Aramco dividends and state oil revenues for deployment capital; structural interest in $80–$100 price range. Current prices above $100 are favorable but PIF has signaled it is reviewing investment commitments, not adding new oil bets—a passive bullish observer rather than an active price mover.
Near-term deadline: No discrete Brent-specific deadline; general fiscal year review ongoing.

### Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA)
Bias: bullish | Confidence: low
Reason: ADNOC revenue disruption is negative for ADIA's sponsoring state; ADIA is inferred to prefer higher oil prices to restore Abu Dhabi's fiscal position. However, ADIA does not trade Brent futures directly—its influence on price is indirect through energy equity allocations and sovereign bond flows.
Near-term deadline: None identified for direct Brent market impact within 7 days.

### Vitol / Trafigura / Glencore
Bias: neutral (hedged both ways) | Confidence: medium
Reason: Physical traders are structurally hedged—they earn on spread and basis, not on outright direction. However, in a backwardated market, their incentive is to sell forward (short futures, long physical), which adds downward pressure to deferred contracts. Their logistical costs from rerouting tankers around the Cape of Good Hope also add ~$4–$6/bbl to physical differentials.
Near-term deadline: Spot cargo-fixing window for May loading (ICE Brent May contract approaching expiry context); physical market activity peaks in the 2-week window before first-notice day.

### Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA)
Bias: bullish | Confidence: low
Reason: As a revenue-stabilization fund for Kuwait, KIA benefits from high prices; KPC revenues are disrupted by Hormuz closure. KIA may be liquidating diversified equity holdings to offset fiscal shortfall—this is price-neutral to mildly price-negative for risk assets but does not directly affect Brent futures.
Near-term deadline: None identified within 7 days that would directly affect Brent price.
